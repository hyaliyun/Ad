Ensure Authenticity: Dialogues should sound natural and true to each character's background, education level, and personality. Ensure their word choices and speech patterns reflect their personal histories and relationships with other characters. 2. Balance Exposition: Be cautious about using dialogue as the sole means of conveying exposition. Strive for a balance between show and tell. Utilize visual storytelling and let character actions reveal plot points as much as their words do. 3. Vary Sentence Structure: Mix up the sentence lengths and styles to maintain an engaging pace. Short, punchy lines can convey urgency or tension, while longer, flowing sentences can be used for introspection or narrative depth. 4. Rhythm and Subtext: Dialogue should have a rhythm that matches the scene's tone, and employing subtext can add layers to character interactions. Think about what the characters might be implying or feeling without explicitly saying it. 5. Character Voice Distinction: Ensure that each character has a distinct voice that sets them apart from others. Characters should not all sound the same. Their word choice, tone, and rhythm should be as unique as their personalities. 6. Conflict and Goals: Good dialogue often stems from the interplay of character goals and conflicts. Each scene should have clear character intentions and obstacles, which should come through in their dialogue choices. 7. Read Aloud and Edit: Read dialogue aloud to see how it flows and sounds. Sometimes dialogues that look good on paper are awkward when spoken. Actors should be able to deliver the lines naturally. 8. Pacing: Make sure dialogue serves the pacing of the story. Some scenes may require swift, back-and-forth exchanges, while others benefit from slower, more thoughtful speeches. 9. Avoiding On-the-Nose Dialogue: Characters shouldn't always say exactly what they mean or feel. Employ subtlety, metaphor, or indirect responses to create richer interactions. 10. Editing for Brevity and Impact: Dialogues should be concise and impactful. Trim unnecessary words or lines that don't push the story forward or develop characters. Find ways to reveal necessary information naturally through character interaction or visual storytelling. 3. **Subtext:** Good dialogue often has layers of meaning. What characters say shouldn't always be taken at face value; there should be underlying motives and emotions. 4. **Brevity:** Ensure that dialogue is concise and impactful. Long-winded speeches should be broken up or distilled to their essence unless the format of a monologue is warranted by the context. 5. **Realism vs. Style:** Depending on your genre, you may opt for hyper-realistic dialogue or something more stylized. Ensure that the style is consistent and suits the story. 6. **Conflict and Goals:** Characters should actively pursue their objectives in conversation. Even in cooperative dialogue, differing methods and beliefs can generate interesting dynamics. 7. **Humor and Levity:** Even in serious scripts, moments of lightness can endear characters to the audience and provide relief from tension. 8. **Readability:** Script dialogue should be easy to read and understand. This means avoiding overly complex sentence structures and obscure vocabulary that might trip up actors. 9. **Implications:** Think about the broader implications of what's being said. How does it affect the characters' relationships, the plot, and the themes of your script? 10. **Revisions:** Revising is a critical part of writing. Read your dialogue out loud, workshop it with others, and be open to making changes.
